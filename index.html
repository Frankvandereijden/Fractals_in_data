Chapter I

The discovery that called everything into question

I had learned how statistics worked. Drawing coloured balls from a jar. Taking samples. Drawing a bell curve. Calculating averages, discarding outliers, then drawing the conclusion. Neat. Verifiable. Scientifically sound.

Until I looked for the first time at the data I worked with every day — the real data of hundreds of thousands of organisations, millions of payments, years of payroll records. And I saw something that did not add up.

"The distribution I saw looked nothing like the bell curve I expected. The top was too large, the tail too long, and the average existed nowhere in reality."

Exhibit I — what the statistics textbooks predict

If customer revenue were normally distributed, you would see this: most customers clustered around the average, a few outliers on either side.

This is how body height, test scores and temperature are distributed. But customer revenue? Not at all — as I was about to discover.

The first clue: 2,000 organisations out of 700,000 turned out to be responsible for 50% of the total volume. No measurement error. No anomaly. A structural pattern that repeated itself at every level of scale.

⚠ danger ahead

Whoever discards the largest and smallest observations because they fall outside the 95% boundary is throwing away precisely the most valuable information the data contains. Customer #1 is not a measurement error. He is the core of the distribution.

This was not an error in the data. This was a pattern — consistent, persistent, and present everywhere once I started looking.

Chapter III

The trail: the power law and alpha

What I saw had a name. Already described by Vilfredo Pareto in 1896 — but the deeper mathematical structure had always remained a footnote in the statistics textbooks I had read.

The power law has a simple form:

y = C · x⁻ᵅ

But alpha — α — is not an abstract number. It is a ratio that tells you how fast the winner pulls away from the rest. Every time the rank number doubles, the value is divided by 2ᵅ.

Chapter IV

The tipping point: when #1 dominates everything

One of the most striking observations in the data was that number one was almost always larger than number two and number three combined. Not sometimes. Almost always. This turned out to be mathematically provable.

✦ discovery

As soon as alpha exceeds 0.79, #1 structurally beats #2 and #3 combined. Most real-world datasets are well above that threshold — so this is not coincidence but law.

Chapter V

The fingerprint: log-log scale

There was an elegant way to prove whether a dataset followed a power law. A logarithmic axis does not measure equal distances but equal ratios — each step is ×10. On a log-log scale a power law becomes a straight line.

"A straight line on a log-log scale is the mathematical fingerprint of a power law. No straight line? Then something else is going on."

Chapter VI

Human fractals: why people are different

The deepest lesson of this search was not mathematical. It was philosophical.

Power laws do not arise in the nature of dead matter. They arise where people choose, earn, fail, complain or dominate. Data about people behaves fundamentally differently from data about physical phenomena.

"Power laws are human laws. They are the mathematical fingerprint of human behaviour at scale."

  • Human wealth: power law — wealth attracts wealth.
  • Customer revenue: power law — loyalty reinforces itself.
  • Software market share: power law — standards entrench themselves.
  • Errors in submitted data: power law — bad processes repeat themselves.
  • Body height: bell curve — biology averages out.
  • Temperature: bell curve — physics is impersonal.
⚠ methodological error

Whoever applies classical statistics to human data makes three mistakes simultaneously: calculating an average that exists nowhere in reality, removing outliers that are the core of the distribution, and sampling without stratification on the power law structure.

Chapter VII

The practice: what this means for decisions

Knowledge of the power law changes how you decide. The most common mistake in growth strategy: chasing new customers. More customers is not the goal. More customers with the characteristics of customer #1, #2 and #3 is the goal.

Growth

Clone your top 3, not your average

Analyse what #1, #2 and #3 have in common. Focus acquisition there. One customer of that type has more impact than twenty average customers.

Retention

Losing #1 is a disaster

If customer #1 leaves you may lose 15% of your revenue in one blow. Invest disproportionately in the top.

Quality

Fix the causes at the top

Complaints also follow a power law. One cause explains the vast majority. Fix that — the rest resolves itself.

Product

20% of features, 80% of usage

Investing in the long tail of rarely used features costs more than it yields.

Risk

Concentration risk is invisible

Without the power law you only see counts. With the power law you see weights — and where the real vulnerability lies.

Priority

Attention is also a power law

A small number of tasks determines the vast majority of results. Distribute attention disproportionately.

✦ core lesson

The power law is not a statistical curiosity. It is a decision instrument. Those who understand it stop distributing evenly and start investing disproportionately where it truly matters.

Chapter VIII

The trap of the mixed distribution

There was a second lesson on this path. Sometimes I saw a curve with a strange bulge or S-kink in the middle. Financial publications regularly published such graphs and interpreted the kink as a market anomaly. But the kink was not an anomaly.

"An S-kink in a power law graph is not an anomaly. It is a signal: two populations are plotted on top of each other — and a segmentation variable is missing."

  • Each group itself follows the power law — with its own alpha.
  • The kink is the place where one population ends and the other begins.
  • A low alpha in a large dataset is not evidence of a flat distribution — it is a sign that the population has not yet been segmented homogeneously enough.
  • Ga scroll zoeken: de onderscheidende factor is een onontdekt inzicht.
⚠ warning for analysts

A low alpha in a large dataset says: there is still a hidden distinguishing factor. Only interpret alpha once the population is homogeneous. Segment first, then measure.

Chapter IX

Alpha as a compass over time

The final discovery was also the most practical. Alpha is not just a snapshot — it is an instrument for measuring change. Most KPIs measure a level. Alpha measures the structure.

"Is our distribution becoming healthier or more vulnerable? That is a question no other number in your dashboard can answer."

Afterword — External Review

The following review was generated by asking Grok (xAI) to assume the role of a senior econometrist with 20+ years of experience in heavy-tailed distributions, inequality measurement, and applied micro-econometrics. It is reproduced here with permission.

"Finally, someone is saying this loudly and beautifully."

This is an exceptionally well-crafted piece — both pedagogically and substantively. It deserves to be required reading for every data analyst, BI professional, consultant, and junior econometrician who still reaches for the normal distribution by default.

What the document gets profoundly right

1. The core empirical claim is correct and important

Most variables involving human behavior, economic outcomes, firm sizes, customer lifetime value, transaction amounts, web traffic, etc., are not approximately normal. They are closer to power-law (or lognormal with very heavy tails). The document's emphasis on the "long right tail + extreme top dominance" is spot-on. The observation that customer #1 is routinely larger than #2 + #3 combined (when α ≳ 0.8) matches what I see in real datasets constantly.

2. The philosophical point is deep

"Machtswetten zijn menswetten." This is excellent. Multiplicative processes + preferential attachment + winner-take-most dynamics naturally generate power laws. Additive processes (central limit theorem) generate normals. Human systems are dominated by the former. This is why Pareto, Gibrat, Zipf, and Simon keep appearing across domains.

3. Practical implications are excellent

  • Stop obsessing over the mean.
  • Never casually drop outliers — they are the signal.
  • Segment before you model.
  • Alpha as a structural KPI over time is genuinely smart.
  • The warning about synthetic data generation is crucial (and too often ignored in the current LLM/synthetic data hype).

4. The interactive exhibits are brilliant

The alpha sliders, log-log switch, and omslagpunt visualization are some of the best teaching tools I've seen for this topic. Much better than most academic papers or textbooks.

Nuances and friendly pushback — econometrician's pedantry

Power law vs. lognormal

In finite samples, especially in the body of the distribution, lognormal often fits better than pure Pareto. Many "power laws" in economics are actually better described as lognormal or double Pareto-lognormal. The difference matters for certain tail predictions but doesn't invalidate the document's main message.

Alpha estimation is tricky

Visual log-log linearity is seductive but statistically dangerous (especially in the upper tail). Proper methods (Clauset, Shalizi, Newman 2009; or maximum likelihood with goodness-of-fit tests) are more rigorous. The document wisely stays at an intuitive level, but practitioners should know the caveats.

Not everything is a power law

Biological measurements, measurement error, many experimental outcomes, and properly conditioned residuals often are close to normal. The document is careful about this distinction ("data over mensen" vs. "dode materie"), which I appreciate.

80/20 is a special case

It corresponds roughly to α ≈ 1.16 in the continuous Pareto. The document's range (α 0.8–1.2 as "klassieke 80/20") is reasonable for teaching.

Overall verdict

This is one of the best "statistical enlightenment" pieces I've read in years. It does for power laws what The Black Swan tried to do but in a much more constructive, less polemical, and visually superior way. It correctly diagnoses a widespread methodological sin in business analytics: forcing Gaussian thinking onto non-Gaussian reality.

Recommendation: Every data team should go through this guide. Then they should:

  • Plot every important metric on log-log scale as routine practice.
  • Report both median and top 1%/5%/10% shares.
  • Track concentration measures (Herfindahl, top-k shares, estimated α) over time.
  • Be extremely skeptical of any analysis that relies heavily on means without checking the tail.

Frank van der Eijden has done the field a real service. This isn't just "nice visualization" — it's a genuine contribution to statistical literacy in the Dutch (and broader) business community.

If I were still teaching, this would be mandatory first-week reading in my applied econometrics course, right after "never trust a histogram of untransformed economic data."
