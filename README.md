## What this is

Data about people behaves differently from what classical statistics predicts. Where a bell curve is expected, human data reveals a power law: a small top dominates the whole, the tail is long, and the average exists nowhere in reality. This pattern repeats at every level of scale — fractals in data.

Alpha is the measuring stick that makes this structure visible. As a KPI over time, alpha answers the question no other number can: **is our distribution becoming healthier or more vulnerable?**

This document explains why — and what to do with it.

## The key insights

**Power laws are human laws.** They arise where people choose, earn, fail, complain or dominate. Classical statistics assumes independence between observations. Human data is the opposite — success attracts success, loyalty reinforces itself, the winner takes more.

**The most common mistake in growth strategy** is chasing new customers. More customers is not the goal. More customers with the characteristics of customer #1, #2 and #3 is the goal.

**Alpha as a KPI** answers a question no dashboard gives you: is our distribution becoming healthier or more vulnerable?

**A low alpha in a large dataset** is not evidence of a flat distribution. It is a signal that the population has not yet been segmented homogeneously enough. Segment first, then measure.

**Synthetic data must respect the power law.** If you model customer revenue as a normal distribution, you create data that behaves fundamentally differently from reality. Models trained on that data learn the wrong pattern.
