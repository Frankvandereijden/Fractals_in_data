The Power Law Problem
in Synthetic Data

Most synthetic data generation tools preserve the mean and the variance. None of them preserve the alpha. That is why models trained on synthetic data fail at the edges — which is precisely where the most important decisions happen.

The core problem

Your synthetic data does not behave like reality

Synthetic data generation is one of the fastest growing areas in AI. Privacy-preserving, scalable, and increasingly used to train production models. But there is a systematic flaw in how almost all synthetic data is currently generated — and it is almost never discussed.

When a synthetic data tool models a column like customer revenue, transaction size, or claim amount, it almost always fits a normal distribution to the observed data. Mean, variance, done. The synthetic data looks statistically similar at the centre. But at the edges — where the most important cases live — it is completely wrong.

The root cause

Human data follows a power law, not a bell curve

Classical statistics is built on drawing balls from a jar. Independent. No memory. Mechanical. The central limit theorem tells us that when many independent random variables are added together, the result converges to a normal distribution.

But human data does not work this way. Customer behaviour, transaction sizes, fraud amounts, claim values — these are the result of multiplicative processes where success attracts success, loyalty reinforces itself, and the winner takes more. The correct model is a power law:

y = C · x⁻ᵅ

The exponent alpha (α) is the measuring stick that quantifies how concentrated the distribution is. It tells you how fast the top pulls away from the rest. And it is the one structural property that current synthetic data tools almost universally ignore.

"Most synthetic data preserves the mean and the variance. None of it preserves the alpha. That is the gap between statistical similarity and behavioural reality."

⚠ governance implication

If a government organisation uses synthetic data generated without power law preservation to train a fraud detection or benefits allocation model, the model will systematically underperform on the highest-value cases. This is not a technical edge case — it is a predictable, systematic governance failure.

The solution

What correct synthetic data generation requires

Fixing this is not technically complex — but it requires knowing the problem exists. The core principle is simple: before generating any column, test whether it follows a power law. If it does, fit the power law parameters rather than a normal distribution.

  • Identify power law columns first. Plot on log-log scale. A straight line is the fingerprint of a power law. Test before assuming normal.
  • Estimate alpha correctly. Visual inspection is a starting point. For production use, apply maximum likelihood estimation (Clauset, Shalizi, Newman 2009) with goodness-of-fit testing.
  • Segment before modelling. A mixed distribution — two populations plotted as one — will show a distorted alpha. Segment by the relevant factor first, then fit each group separately.
  • Validate at the tail, not just the centre. Standard statistical tests check mean and variance. For power law columns, validate that the top 1%, 5%, and 10% share in the synthetic data matches the real data.
  • Track alpha as a quality metric. The alpha of the synthetic distribution should match the alpha of the real distribution within an acceptable tolerance. If it does not, the synthetic data is structurally wrong regardless of how similar the means are.
✦ key principle

Columns that require power law modelling: revenue per customer, transaction amounts, claim sizes, messages per user, website visits per page, organisation size by headcount, error frequency by source.

Columns where normal distribution is appropriate: age, body height, test scores, temperature measurements, biological measurements.

Beyond generation — ongoing governance

Alpha as a structural quality metric for synthetic data

Most synthetic data quality frameworks measure fidelity using statistical distance metrics — KL divergence, Wasserstein distance, column-wise distributions. These metrics test whether the centre of the distribution is preserved. They are largely blind to the tail.

Alpha provides a single, interpretable number that captures what these metrics miss: how concentrated is the distribution, and does the synthetic data match the concentration of reality?

Tracked over time — across generations of synthetic data, across model versions, across data refresh cycles — alpha becomes a structural KPI that answers a question no other quality metric can answer:

"Is our synthetic data becoming a better or a worse reflection of the real-world distribution it is supposed to represent?"

Anankaia perspective

Why this is an AI governance issue, not just a technical one

The power law problem in synthetic data sits at the intersection of technical methodology and institutional risk — which is precisely where AI governance lives.

Three governance dimensions

⚖️
Accountability
When a model trained on structurally incorrect synthetic data makes a wrong decision on a high-value case, who is accountable? The data team that generated the synthetic data? The model team that trained on it? The governance framework must address this chain of responsibility.
🔍
Auditability
Alpha preservation is a measurable, auditable property. Including alpha-matching as a required quality criterion in synthetic data governance frameworks makes the standard concrete and verifiable — not a vague aspiration.
🏛️
Public sector risk
Dutch government organisations processing payroll, benefits, and tax data operate on distributions with very high alpha values — a tiny fraction of organisations represents a majority of volume. Synthetic data generated for these environments without power law preservation is structurally unsuitable for training AI systems that operate on the real data.
